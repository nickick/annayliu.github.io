"OK, Glass. Google where did you come from?"

Anna Liu

Graduating from Duke University. Moving on to tech paradise in Silicon Valley. Forever a blue devil.

When Google was founded, Larry Page and Sergey Brin sought to change the world by improving humanity’s access to information. With the PageRank algorithm, Google took advantage of the interconnected nature of the World Wide Web to create a search engine that would provide even the most obscure knowledge within seconds to people at the touch of their fingertip.

That was over 15 years ago.

Today, Google is a multinational corporation that stretches across many Internet verticals, including search, online advertising, software, and cloud computing. Backed by a well-established technological infrastructure and an extraordinary pool of technical talent, Google now seeks to take another huge leap for mankind: freeing data from computers and mobile devices and placing the world’s information directly in front of the eyes of the viewer.

Welcome Project Glass, a wearable computer with an optical head-mounted display that augments its user’s reality through delivery of real-time data.

At Google I/O 2012, Google Glass (literally) skydived into San Francisco’s Moscone Center and was greeted amidst a sea of thundering applause. By teaming with some of world’s top athletes in skydiving and mountain biking, Google took the product demo of Google Glass to the next level. In the process of doing so, Google also shared an extraordinary, first-hand experience of the possibilities that hands-free, ubiquitous computing can bring to the world.

Powered by Android, Project Glass spurred both excitement and controversy when Google first revealed its concept in April 2012. On the one hand, the possibilities that Glass offered to its user seemed endless: monitoring feeds about the weather, creating instant contact with friends, receiving turn-by-turn to your desired destination, just to name a few. On the other hand, Glass sparked consumer worries regarding privacy concerns and the questionable security of personal information.

Born in Google X, the same lab that incubated the development of futuristic technology such as driverless cars and space elevators, the wearable device was nowhere complete for consumer sales, let alone for public beta, during its initial release. Yet in 2012, the search company made a crucial move in unveiling its Project Glass efforts in order to ignite conversation with future users.

In April 2013, the Google Glass explorer edition was made available for pilot testers and developers in the United States at $1,500. A year later on April 15, 2014, a more “fully-polished” version of the headset was released for consumers at the same price for only one day. In addition, the tech ecosystem for Google Glass, backed by the early adopter Explorer program, appears to thrive, with nine software updates released, 42 Glassware apps developed, iOS support integrated, prescription frames added, and more to date.

During its first year of release, Google Glass has been lauded tremendously as the future of wearable computing, criticized heavily for leading the apocalypse of consumer privacy, and mocked shamelessly for its lack of style and uber-nerd appeal. Because of the ethical and safety problems Glass poses, its users have banned from bars and restaurants, ticketed for distracted driving, and dubbed infamously as ‘Glassholes.’

So what is the verdict for Glass? Is it indeed the future of wearable technology? Or will it forever remain the punch line of geeky jokes?

In this three-fold project, I will

  1. Explore the societal and legal ramifications of Google Glass in respect to ethics and policy

  2. Analyze the technical foundations of Google Glass from a design and development perspective, as well as current Glassware applications

  3. Provide a human-centric perspective of a reality augmented with Glass technology

By doing so, I hope to provide a multifaceted response to the following question:

What is it going to take for Google to obtain the mainstream adoption of Glass?
