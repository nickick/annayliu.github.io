As of this week, your data is not safe on the Internet. Here's why.

Anna Liu

CS & Bio Student at Duke

Earlier this week, a researcher at Google Security and Finnish security firm Codenomicon discovered the Heartbleed bug in OpenSSL, a widely used open-source software that is used to encrypt sensitive information, like emails, passwords and credit card numbers.

Because of the Heartbleed bug, a huge chunk of the Internet is vulnerable to cyber attacks. So with one attack, a hacker can grab 64kb of memory from a server and leave no trace of ever doing so. Imagine how much information can be accessed over repeatable exploits. Scary? No, this is catastrophic.

Apparently, this security flaw in OpenSSL has been around for 2 years, affecting over 2/3 of sites and services, such as Google, Facebook, Amazon, and Yahoo, that depend on OpenSSL for encryption. According to security expert, Bruce Schneier, "On the scale of 1 to 10, this [Heartbleed] is an 11."

The Heartbleed Bug effectively means past SSL traffic can be decrypted, unless it used Perfect Forward Secrecy. http://t.co/PCREwSltRJ ಠ_ಠ

In other words, the Heartbleed bug comprises your privacy online - big time. All your past, present, and future communications on at least half a million sites can be seen by anyone. Even with Perfect Forward Secrecy enabled, your past data is still vulnerable.

As programmers around the world are busy patching up their systems with a bug-free version of OpenSSL, the best thing for you to do to protect your data online is to change your passwords IMMEDIATELY. You can also test for your vulnerability at afflicted sites here.
