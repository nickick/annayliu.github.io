Contemplating About Life After College in 2014

Anna Liu

CS & Bio Student at Duke

A friend of mine recently graduated from college, and will be finishing his last semester abroad. He texted me that studying abroad would mark the beginning of his life after college. He wanted to have a great experience by starting a new chapter in his life, However, he also did not want to leave everything he knew behind, and he was not sure what the consequences would be if he did so.

His thoughts made me reminisce about my childhood. In my twenty-one years of life, I have lived through many “chapters” of life. Most of them were short, consisting of two to three years at most. Almost all started when I attended a new school and ended with my moving to another city. Each time, I picked up my bags and moved, I remembered the feeling of wanting to start anew, to change a little something about myself, and to fashion myself in a completely new way to unknown strangers. In the process, I would leave my past (including childhood friends and a little part of myself) behind.

There were both pros and cons to moving around every so often. It was fun getting the opportunity to reinvent myself. It was exciting getting to live in a new environment. I enjoyed the exhilaration of new people and new places.

Yet, there was no social media back then to help me stay in touch with old childhood friends. I had to rebuild social networks from scratch through in-person communication with real people. I spent so much time meeting and spending time with new people that I forgot to keep in touch with old friends.

I now suffer the consequences of not having been able (and not really making the effort) to stay in touch with old friends from my pre-college days. I do not have a “group” I can come back home to hang out with during winter breaks and summer vacations. I have grown accustomed to the changes that came with moving from one place to another.

In fact, I take pride knowing that my independence is rooted in my ability to easily adapt to new situations and places. I am sociable and outgoing, but part of me always feels lonely. Because I have already encountered so much change in my life, because my life has been jumbled up and pieced back together so many times, when I look do back, I do not have anything concrete to look back to.

This spring will be my last semester at Duke University. After college graduation, I will be moving back to San Francisco, the place where I spent most of my adolescence growing up around the bay. Even though I will be moving back to the place I have learned to call home, my world will once again be uprooted. I am lucky to have my family here, but I will be entering a brave new world of the tech industry. I’m excited. I’m scared. I fear for what lies ahead. But at least I am more emotionally mature to handle whatever life throws at me, and that means not leaving behind whatever remains from my childhood and the happy memories of my college years.
