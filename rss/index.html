Oh, Bitcoin.

"Beware the mania for Bitcoin, the tulip of the 21st century," states Jean-Pierre Landau.

“Bitcoin is evil," decries Paul Krugman.

Financial experts are quick to point out that the recent popularity and excitement surrounding Bitcoin does not necessarily translate into its economic reality.

Yet, Bitcoin disrupts the conventional way people think about and use money. It is not paper-based, but rather Internet-based. Bitcoin, at its core, is a breakthrough in computer science. It provides an unequivocal and secure method for one Internet user to transfer a piece of unique digital property to another Internet user.

The real disruptive power of Bitcoin to our economy and society does not lie in its intrinsic value as a currency, but rather in its underlying platform, the Bitcoin protocol. Bitcoin is the currency of the future because its technological basis enables it to transform one of the fundamental things that people do - make payments.

Bitcoin’s decentralized, peer-to-peer system disrupts how financial institutions process payment.

Bitcoin is a digital bearer instrument that provides a secure, decentralized way to record and transfer ownership rights of money and assets between unrelated parties, without the presence of a trusted intermediary. The Bitcoin protocol is a Big Bang Disruption that runs the world’s first truly open financial network.

The double-spending problem used to plague creators of digital cash: how do you prevent a person from using the same digital coin twice. Before Bitcoin, the only solution was to have a central authority to approve a transaction as final. However, Satoshi Nakamoto, through Bitcoin, was the first to create an electronic system to solve the double-spending problem in a completely, decentralized fashion.

The Bitcoin block chain serves as a decentralized, shared public ledger that, as a whole, keeps track of Bitcoin addresses. Through a safe, transparent, and mathematically secure method, ownership rights to digital money flow directly from the sender’s digital wallet to the receiver’s digital wallet. Bitcoin offers a peer-to-peer system that removes people’s need for a central authority to process monetary transactions when making payments.

Whereas financial institutions heavily safeguard their private ledgers, anyone can validate transactions and ownership of all Bitcoins anytime they want. This ensures not only the security, but also the transparency of financial assets stored in the Bitcoin network. Anyone can buy into the distributed ledger by purchasing one of a fixed number of slots (aka the digital “coins”); owners of these slots can sell out of the ledger anytime, anywhere by trading their Bitcoin to someone else that wants to buy into the ledger. Bitcoin’s public ledger makes transaction fees much, much lower than those of a central bank, and eliminates authorization imposed by a central authority; Bitcoin users can directly buy in or out of the ledger at little to no cost when making payments.

The decentralized nature of the Bitcoin network disrupts the traditional operation of financial institutions. First, there is the impossibility of financial censorship by a single party because the Bitcoin ledger is public. Therefore, all payment transactions are logged and openly displayed. Second, since transactions must be confirmed at least six times by computers across a distributed network, there is no single point of failure. Therefore, payment fraud can be easily detected. Third, unlike a private ledger kept by banks, Bitcoin transactions are completely transparent, allowing for financial openness and promoting trust for Bitcoin as an accepted payment system.

Marc Andreessen asserts that Bitcoin’s future lies in a classic network effect, a positive feedback loop driven by the self-interested participation of four distinct groups: (1) consumers (2) merchants (3) miners and (4) developers and entrepreneurs. Bitcoin miners run the computers that process and validate the transactions that take place across the cryptocurrency’s decentralized network. Anyone with the technical know-how can download the Bitcoin open-source mining software and build their own mining rig. In addition, as their reward, miners can earn 25 bitcoins for solving ever-increasing computational problems that process Bitcoin transactions. The ease of participation on the Bitcoin platform, along with the incentive to process Bitcoin transactions, supports the decentralized network that enables Bitcoin to not only pose a threat to financial institutions, but to also gain popularity as a form of accepted currency and payment system.

Bitcoin as a technological platform drives financial innovation for payment systems.

The more people that use Bitcoin, the more valuable Bitcoin becomes for everyone to use it, providing a higher incentive for the next person to start using the technology. Bitcoin as an evolving technological platform not only makes the digital currency easier for newcomers to use, but transforms money into "smart money". It enables developers and entrepreneurs, a group that is part of Andreessen’s four-sided network effect, to create consumer products and services on top of the Bitcoin platform that have been, until recently, impossible. With more products and services that utilize Bitcoin, the more prevalent Bitcoin becomes as an adopted payment system.

Bitcoin makes money programmable, hence why it is so popular among developers. it is an application programming interface (API) for money that provides the opportunity for developers to create countless financial innovations. Bitcoin not only offers an API for personal accounts and transactions, but makes that API available to coders. As a predominantly, developer-driven technology, Bitcoin gives its users the power to define the usage and control of money, leading to a faster rate of product innovation of Bitcoin as a payment system with broader consumer appeal.

Traditional money do have APIs, but they are closed to regular people. In a traditional financial network, trust between parties is achieved through a complex web of regulations, fraud detection, and accreditation. Financial networks are protected through exclusivity: APIs are closed and inaccessible except to the few that are trusted. For example, Mastercard and Paypal limit who can build applications on top of their platforms and what those applications can do. However, trust in the Bitcoin financial model is distributed across a large and ever-growing network of collaborators who continuously validate each other’s activity. As a result, there is no need for access controls or exclusivity to maintain Bitcoin as a secure way for payment. Everyone can participate in the Bitcoin network and access the APIs.

Silicon Valley venture capital firm, Andreessen Horowitz, has invested in just under 50 million dollars in Bitcoin-related startups and actively searches for more Bitcoin-related investment opportunities. The firm believes that the network effect will carry Bitcoin to dominance as a widespread payment system in the not-so-distant future. One of the most exciting applications include building a Bitcoin platform for international remittance. Because Bitcoin transaction fees are much lower than the amount that banks and payments companies extract from international transactions, switching to Bitcoin will enable immigrants to keep more of the money that they send to their families in their home countries. In addition, Bitcoin presents further opportunities in micropayments, and can be a catalyst that drives the development of modern economic systems in third world countries. These are the types of possibilities that make Bitcoin evangelists so excited about the cryptocurrency and its potential for innovation and widespread adoption as a payment system.

Bitcoin the technology promotes adoption of Bitcoin the currency as a method of payment.

Bitcoin is a digital currency whose value is based on (1) the use of the payment system today and (2) future speculation of the use of the payment system. It is not that the Bitcoin currency has an arbitrary value and people are trading it. Rather, it is because Bitcoin is used for trading that as a result, the cryptocurrency has value. Although the current value of Bitcoin currency is based more on speculation than its actual payment volume, Bitcoin’s value has increased dramatically during recent months because speculation have made more people aware of the reality of its usefulness for payments.

Edward Hadas, Reuters’ economics editor, calls Bitcoin “a Fool’s Gold Standard.” Critics of Bitcoin point out that even though Bitcoin operates like mainstream currencies, it will never attain the real value associated with conventional money because it is not backed by a central bank. They argue that Bitcoin’s volatility not only makes the predictability of its value impossible, but can render them almost worthless. Yet every day, more and more consumers and merchants are buying, using, and selling Bitcoins all around the world. As Bitcoin tools and technology are improved by developers and entrepreneurs, the cryptocurrency becomes easier, and more secure for everyone to use. And as more and more people use Bitcoin, the value of the digital currency will also expand and drive its adoption as a popular payment system among mainstream users.

Critics of Bitcoin also highlight that given the small number of consumers who currently pay with the digital currency, its volatility makes it unappealing to merchants. Yet why would merchants not be interested in a digital alternative for businesses when conventional payment fees consume nearly half of profit margins and conventional payment methods make accepting international payments difficult?

In addition, merchants are attracted to Bitcoin because its technological protocol eliminates the risk of credit card fraud. The receiver of a payment through Bitcoin does not get any information from the sender that can be used to steal money from the sender in the future. The digital currency’s technological basis in cryptography protects the private information of receivers and senders, while its distributed ledger ensures the validity of all payment transactions taking place in the network. Bitcoin is different from conventional methods of electronic payment because it puts the liability of the payment on the payer. Because merchants know that payments via Bitcoin are coming directly from the buyer and that payments via Bitcoin from the buyer are irreversible, merchants can increase their profit margins by accepting Bitcoin for payment.

One thing that both critics and developers agree on is that Bitcoin’s number one issue is security. On February 7, 2014, Mt.Gox, the Japan-based exchange that once held 80% of the world’s Bitcoin trades, halted withdrawals of the digital currency due to transaction malleability, an attack that changes the unique ID of a Bitcoin transaction before it is confirmed on the Bitcoin network. Critics were quick to highlight the shutdown of Mt.Gox and the subsequent drop in Bitcoin prices as a sign of the ‘cryptocalypse’, the end of the Bitcoin world as we know it. Yet despite the media hype of Mt. Gox, it is easy to forget that Bitcoin is a nascent technology and that fraud can occur on multiple fronts to other Bitcoin services as well. Developers are aware that Bitcoin presents some of the most interesting and challenging computer science problems to solve right now, and that multifaceted solutions are required to prevent future fraud.

From a technological standpoint, Bitcoin’s transaction malleability problems are no more than fixable software bugs, common to all emerging software technologies. Although a major Bitcoin exchange has been shut down, transaction malleability does not break the core of Bitcoin operation. Its decentralized network forms the crux of the digital currency’s appeal to not only developers, miners, and entrepreneurs, but also to consumers who pay with Bitcoin and merchants who accept Bitcoin.

Bitcoin is for the people.

In truth, only time can tell Bitcoin’s viability as a long-term currency. However, its future as the go-to payment system of tomorrow is brighter than ever before. Because it operates as a distributed, peer-to-peer system that promotes financial transparency, Bitcoin eliminates the need for financial institutions as a third party to confirm payment transactions. Its basis as an evolving technology platform provide countless opportunities for developer and entrepreneurs to redefine conventional methods of payment. Lastly, Bitcoin the technology drives adoption of Bitcoin the currency as an accepted form of payment among merchants and consumers because it is circulated directly between buyers and sellers, and requires no authorization and little to no transaction fees.

In an allusion to the famous quote from Abe Lincoln’s Gettysburg Address, Bitcoin, especially the technology as a form of payment, is “of the people”, “by the people”, and “for the people.” Who knows if it will ultimately “perish from the earth”? But from where it stands today, Bitcoin has no chance of doing so anytime in the near future. And if you need proof that Bitcoin is what Gavin Andresen, chief-scientist of Bitcoin Foundation, calls the “decentralized currency of the people”, have you heard of the Auburn fan who racked up over $24,000 in Bitcoin through ESPN?

China goes tech-saavy.

In the 1970s, and especially during the 1990s, foreign direct investments have improved the quality of the Chinese high technology industry. In the 21st century, how is China developing its indigenous technological industry to support its competitiveness in world markets? Let us examine the country's smartphone, social media, and e-commerce markets to address these two questions.

In China, there are smartphones everywhere. Literally everywhere.

Umeng, a Chinese analytics firm, confirmed that, by the end of 2013, the number of active smartphones in China exceeded 700 million. Because China has the world's largest number of active smartphone users, many domestic and international companies want to grab a slice of this lucrative market.

Xiaomi's track record of rapid growth and saavy marketing indicates that this Chinese-based company will quickly become a mighty competitor in the US smartphone market.

The dream of Xiaomi's CEO, Lei Jun is to build a smartphone company that can compete with Apple. Despite China's reputation for manufacturing low-cost, low-quality goods, Lei Jun wants to push the idea that China has the ability to manufacture high-end hardware.

Xiaomi challenges Samsung in the Chinese smartphone marketplace. In the later half of 2013, Xiaomi's Mi 2S was the most popular smartphone in the Chinese market, followed by Samsung's Galaxy S4.

Xiaomi's successful sales can be contributed to the company's willingness to try alternative marketing strategies. By utilizing social media to gather user feedback, Xiaomi has also gained a loyal fanbase. Yet, Xiaomi still needs to prove the competitive advantage of its smartphones on the international market.

The Chinese love online shopping.

According to McKinsey, China has the world's second largest e-commerce market, with estimates as high as $210 billion for revenues in 2012 and a compound annual growth rate of 120% since 2003. Almost 90% of Chinese electronic retailing occurs on virtual marketplaces. These sprawling e-commerce platforms offer products and services to consumers.

Alibaba is China's leading e-commerce company, operating the world's largest marketplace for both international and domestic Chinese trade. This company is focused on fostering an open, collaborative, and prosperous e-commerce system that benefits consumers, merchants, and the economy.

Alibaba wants to become a more global company. It has already changed the way China shops，and also wants to transform the country's service industry, adding even more users to the company's group of 300 million customers. Alibaba has its sights set firmly on the US market, establishing an investment organization to expand the company's overseas influence and competitiveness. This year, the company has begun the process of an initial public offering in the United States, with economists valuing the company at $110 billion.

If Alibaba has seemed unstoppable in its 15-year rise, an IPO will could make one of the world's most important high-tech companies face its most serious challenges so far. Alibaba's strategy of building its own global e-commerce empire, with its own financial services, has attracted close scrutiny from China's regulators and resistance from the country's banks. Alibaba's executive vice chairman says that the company's strategy has a single focus: to boost Alibaba's core e-commerce platform, especially as consumers shop more on smartphones.

More than 300 million people use social medial including blogs, social networks, microblogs, online communities, etc. China's Internet users spend more than 40% of their time online for social media, a number that continues to rise rapidly.

Blogging began in 2004, and a year later, social networks with chatting capabilities, such as Ren Ren, were established. In 2009, Sina Weibo launched microblogging with multimedia service.

This explosive growth shows few signs of abating. It is harder for the Chinese government to censor social media than other information channels. Because many Chinese citizens are somewhat skeptical of the authority of governments and formal institutions, users disproportionately direct more respect towards opinion leaders on social media.

Sina Weibo is among China's fastest growing and most talked about social media, often referred to as China's Twitter and Facebook. In just four short years, Weibo has attracted over 500 million users, including ordinary people, celebrities, and other public figures, as well as organizations, such as media, businesses, governments, and charities.

However, Weibo confines itself in China, having amassed a huge user base in China and Chinese communities abroad. Besides Sina introducing an English-version of Weibo and allowing users to login through Facebook, Weibo has made no effort in expanding to foreign markets, rather focusing on recruiting users within China. New research suggests that Sina may need to re-consider its strategy: Weibo seems to have peaked in popularity. Weibo's domestic competitor, Tencent's WeChat, poses a threat to Weibo's competitiveness in the social media market.

Tencent's Wechat was first launched in late 2011. During its first two years, WeChat already accumulated over 300 million years. It approaches a completely different strategy than Weibo: going directly to the international market to sell its product. However, Weibo is very massive and popular in China, and also serves as the most active and powerful Chinese platform for public expression.

This year, Sina Weibo fundraised $500 million in preparation for its IPO. Weibo also has the support of Alibaba in the social media market. Alibaba holds an 18% stake in Weibo, having stopped the use of WeChat seller-side services, and allowing Weibo users to directly buy products from Taobao. In addition, from an international perspective, China's Weibo has a greater influence than United-States based Twitter. In fact, Twitter worries that its Chinese counterpart might actually impede its international growth.

"Made in China" looks like a tech winner.

From the Chinese market perspective of smartphones, e-commerce, and social media, the growth of China's tech industry has been quick and mighty. Web-based products have also been well received by Chinese citizens, enabling domestic businesses to hold a significant market share. Indigenous products are fierce competitors with their foreign counterparts, and Chinese companies, such as Alibaba, have invested heavily in indigenous products to expand their overseas influence and competitiveness.

In terms of the smartphone, e-commerce and social media markets, the Chinese economy and its high-tech sector have not only maintained their rapid growth, but through their clever marketing, design, and business strategies, have also captured international acclaim. However, on the increasingly competitive international market, Chinese companies must devote more sources to research and development to keep up and surpass their foreign rivals.

Chinese tech companies need to realize that their local knowledge is a source of competitive advantage, and that they must take advantage of this expertise. Companies with global aspirations need to develop an international brand to promote high-quality and reliable products.

At the end of 2013, China's Internet population reached 618 million, with 81% through mobile. An increasingly connected country provides more opportunities for tech innovation in the likes of Alibaba, Xiaomi and Sina Weibo.

]]>http://annayliu.com/2014/04/10/hack-the-mind-by-loving/26c11a16-ed59-4251-b3f1-0366eb2c05e4Thu, 10 Apr 2014 22:09:47 GMT<![CDATA[In Cryptography We Trust? Bitcoin? WTF?]]>My little Bitcoin 101. This cryptocurrency is pretty darn intriguing.

What is currency?

Currency refers to the fact or quality of being generally accepted or in use. Specifically, the term is synonymous with money in any form that is used or circulated as a viable medium of exchange.

What is Bitcoin?

Bitcoin is the world’s first decentralized digital currency that relies on cryptography to provide instantaneous peer-to-peer transactions anywhere in the world. Satoshi Nakamoto published the specification for the open-source platform in 2009. Bitcoin’s design is public, nobody owns or controls the virtual currency, and everyone can take part in its development and use.

Why are people going nuts about Bitcoin?

There are many advantages to Bitcoin. Its transactions require no bank or clearing house approval. It can be used anywhere as long as you have internet access. Your Bitcoin account cannot be frozen as a result of governmental action. And there are no prerequisites or arbitrary limits associated with opening and maintaining a Bitcoin account.

How do I start using Bitcoin?

Download a Bitcoin wallet! A Bitcoin wallet allows you to transact with other users and gives you ownership of a balance with which you can send and receive Bitcoins. You can install a Bitcoin wallet on your phone, tablet, and/or computer. Desktop wallets give you complete control over your wallet, but you are responsible for protecting your money and performing backups. Mobile wallets allow you to bring Bitcoin with you on-the-go. Web-based wallets allow you to use Bitcoin on any browser or mobile and may provide additional services and support as well.

Once you have your Bitcoin wallet installed, it will generate your first Bitcoin address. You can let your friends know of your Bitcoin address so they can pay you and vice versa. It is like how email works, but Bitcoin addresses are only meant to be used once. Don’t worry, you can always generate another Bitcoin addresses when you need it.

How does a Bitcoin transaction work?

The Bitcoin network relies on the block chain, a shared public ledger that includes all confirmed transactions. Users rely on the block chain to calculate their Bitcoin balance and to verify new transactions. Cryptography is employed to enforce the integrity and  chronology of the block chain.

A Bitcoin transaction involves a transfer of values between Bitcoin wallets that gets included in the block chain. Bitcoin wallets maintain a private key used to sign transactions, providing mathematical proof that they have come from the owner of the wallet. The signature also prevents transactions from being altered by anyone else once they have been issued.

How are Bitcoin transactions processed?

Bitcoin transactions are processed through mining, which involves utilizing computer hardware to do mathematical calculations for the Bitcoin network. These calculations are used to confirm waiting transactions that get included in the block chain.

Confirmation means that a transaction has been processed and is highly unlikely to be reversible. In order for a transaction to be confirmed, transactions must be packed into a block that fits very strict cryptographic rules, which must then be verified by the network.

As a reward, Bitcoin miners can collect transaction fees, as well as newly created Bitcoins, for their efforts. Bitcoin mining is a specialized and competitive market where miners are rewarded in proportion to the amount of work they’ve done. Not all Bitcoin users are Bitcoin miners. In fact, the mining market has become quite saturated as Bitcoin prices continue to surge.

Don’t judge a book by its cover. Let’s apply this statement to digital interfaces: mobile apps, websites, web applications…anything that you find on a screen. It’s wrong.

For a digital product, its “cover” defines its identity. The interface is the only viable means of communication between the designer and the user. Therefore, it is of the utmost importance that the designer creates an interface with obvious purpose and utility. The best interfaces are simple, minimalist, and intuitive: one that avoids feature creeps and provides the user with a fluid, painless experience.

Failure to do so results in the user forming an incorrect mental conception of the product, leading to confusion, errors, and decrease levels of user engagement. In fact, if the designer fails to convey the value proposition of the product through the user interface, how is the business expected to generate revenue, let alone retain customers?

In his book, The Design of Everyday Things, Donald A. Norman describes four crucial aspects of physical product design that can easily be extended to the design of digital interfaces.

  1. Natural Mapping: Take advantage of physical analogies and cultural standards to convey the relationship between a control and its resulting function.

  2. Perceived Affordances: Utilize the perceived action of a device or object to emphasize its intended action.

  3. Feedback: After a user interacts with the interface, be sure to present the effect of the action in a clear and concise format.

  4. Constraints: Incorporate forcing functions to prevent errors that may arise from user experience.

Bottom line: Simplicity and ease of use could determine the “make it or break it” point of your digital product.

]]>http://annayliu.com/2013/12/23/an-aside-on-user-experience/bd61ec37-dfa8-459d-bb99-0a87b16602f0Tue, 24 Dec 2013 03:40:18 GMT<![CDATA[Modern Musical Innovations of the 20th Century (part two)]]>In part two of this post series, we will explore the modern musical innovations rooted in the curation and juxtaposition of diverse instrumental and electronic sounds. These six composers sought to liberate sound in all its beauty and aesthetic glory, enabling music to achieve an entirely novel form of sonic freedom.

1. Aaron Copland: Hoedown from Rodeo (1942)

During the 1930s, the musical genre of jazz was experiencing a tremendous period of creativity, yet American composers were still searching for an authentic American style. Copland attempts to capture the American spirit in Hoedown from Rodeo. His concern with the role of a composer in society led him to not only write sonatas for violin, piano, and other orchestra instruments, but also a number of contemporary musical currents, big ballets, and symphonies using American folklore and American subjects.

2. Elliot Carter: Sonata for Cello (1948)

Following the death of Anton Webern, serial music became much more disseminated, and was widely viewed as the "path to new music." Total serialism is the radical notion that the composer applies the notion of series to not only pitch but also to successions of rhythms, dynamics, and timbre. In addition, the advent of electronic means of music production enabled the precise control of impossible complex rhythms and shifts of timbre. Although Carter initially composed mature pieces in the neoclassical tonality, he eventually embarked on a career of marked musical innovation. He gave attention to the technical features of his music, particularly metric modulation, which involves gradual changes in tempo, eventually resulting in its constant fluctuation. The first movement of Sonata for Cello illustrates Carter's emphasis on atonality and rhythmic complexity; musically speaking, as the piano walks, the cello swims.

3. Charles Ives: The Unanswered Question (1906, revision between 1930-1935)

The Unanswered Question is an exemplary work of collage music in which Ives showcases his ability to marry the consonant with the dissonant. In the background of slow quiet strings that represent "The Silence of the Druids," a solo trumpet poses the "The Perennial Question of Existence" to which a woodwind quartet of "Fighting Answers" vainly attempts to provide an answer, growing more frustrated and agitated. When the woodwinds finally give up, the trumpet poses the question silently one last time, its sound fading into the backdrop of slow, solemn lines of the strings. The Unanswered Question heavily influenced John Adam's On the Transmigration of Souls, which will explored next.

4. John Adams: On the Transmigration of Souls (2002)

Similar to Ives' The Unanswered Question, Adam's On the Transmigration of Souls is a college piece that effortlessly blends in the sounds of orchestra, chorus, children's choir, and pre-recorded tape. The Pulitzer-winning composition, initially commissioned by the New York Philharmonic, serves to create a "memory space", a place where listeners can go and be alone with their thoughts and emotions. Although it was written to commemorate the 9/11 attacks, Adam believes that the piece summons a human experience that goes beyond this particular tragic event in history. The word 'transmigration' in this piece does not only point to the transition from the living to the dead, but also the change which took place within the souls that were left behind, of those who suffer pain and loss when accepting the passing of their loved ones. Adam was heavily influenced by Ives when he created the spiritual model that embodies this particular piece of composition. On the Transmigration of Souls does not exploit emotions, but instead concentrates on the loss and grief experienced by those left behind to deal with the ramifications of the 9/11 tragedy.

5. Karlheinz Stockhausen: Gesang der Junglinge (1956)

The advent of novel musical technologies for sound synthesis permitted not only the introduction of new musical instruments but also paved the way for new ways of experiencing music. Stockhausen's Gesang der Junglinge uses both synthesized sounds and modified recordings of a boy soprano voice in serialized levels of intelligibility. He believed that the direction and movement of the sounds is shaped by the musician, whose function is to open up a new dimension in musical experience through the liberation of sound. Gesang der Junglinge is a composition, that for the first time, brought together the two opposing worlds of purely electronically generated German Elektronische Musik and the French Musique Concrete.

6. Gyorgy Ligeti: Poeme Symphonique for 100 Metronomes (1962) and Etudes, Book I, Fanfares (1985)

Although the middle period of Ligeti's works clearly demonstrated the lessons of the electronic studio being applied to instrumental music, his later innovations revealed his pursuit of the avant-garde. On the one hand, his Poeme Symphonique for 100 Metronomes showcases his musical experimentations in the Fluxus movement, which sought to blend different artistic media and disciplines. In the performance of this composition, metronomes are set up on a performance platform, all are wound up to their maximum extent and set to different speeds. Once fully wound, there is a silence of two to six minutes, then at the conductor's signal, they are all started as simultaneously as possible. As the metronomes wind down one after another and stop, periodicity becomes noticeable in the sound, and individual metronomes can be clearly distinguished. The piece typically ends with just one metronome ticking alone for a few beats, and then is followed by silence. On the other hand, Ligeti's Etudes is regarded as one of the most creative achievements of his last decades, and one of the most significant sets of 20th century piano studies, combining virtuoso technical problems with expressive content. In Fanfares, the melody and accompaniment frequently exchanges roles in a unique polyrhythmic fashion.
