Arnold Schoenberg's Pierrot Lunaire

Anna Liu

Graduating from Duke University. Moving on to tech paradise in Silicon Valley. Forever a blue devil.

The stanzas in Schoenberg's melodrama Pierrot Lunaire all follow the typical 13 line rondelle form. In Moondrunk, the couplet, "The wine that through the eyes is drunk, at night the moon pours down in tourrents," appears twice in the poem, where the first line of the couplet is both the first and last line of the poem. The couplet in Columbine, "The moon's pallid blossoms, the white and wonderous roses" also follows the pattern described in Moondrunk. However, despite the repetition of lines in the poem, Schoenberg rarely employs the same music when the same line appears again, and in doing so, he breaks away from the traditional standards and sounds in composition and music respectively, thus enabling his music to possess spontaneous, evocative and expressive characteristics.

For example, when Moondrunk opens with the couplet, the music is lighthearted, the notes that the singer projects are within close range of each other, and the singer's words are enunciated clearly; thus this gives listeners a sense that the character depicted in the melodrama still retains most of his sober behavior and may just be slightly tipsy from the moonlight. Between the first two stanzas, the music becomes more playful and picks up in pace, suggesting that as the character continues to bask in the moonlight, he begins to feel the effects of intoxication as thoughts and emotions whirl about in his head. When the couplet is repeated, the singer's pitch begins to widen and most words are still enunciated clearly until the last slurred word "torrents," perhaps emphasizing that the character has now entered a completely inebriated stage. In the last stanza, the singer rapidly changes between spoken word and singing and the music becomes wild, and in the last line, the singer's vocals show that the main character no longer has his thoughts straight and entered into a drunk stupor.

Columbine indicates that the character has become sexually aroused while under the intoxication of moonlight. When the music to Columbine begins on a romantic tone, from the first stanza, the listener can infer that the main character is desiring a virgin. The music gradually shifts to a more mysterious and dark tone in the second stanza, perhaps evoking the erotic thoughts racing through the main character's mind while he fantasizes on his sexual desires. The repetition of the couplet in the second stanza suggests that the character's desire for a pure and innocent sexual partner has intensified. The music in the third stanza continues to emphasize the main character's desires, but also suggests that his desire for a virgin is nothing but a fantasy generated from his inebriation from the moonlight.
