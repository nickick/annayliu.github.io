Bela Bartok's Suite, Opus 14

Anna Liu

Graduating from Duke University. Moving on to tech paradise in Silicon Valley. Forever a blue devil.

Bartok's Suite, Opus 14 is not only a reflection of his mastery in expression but also his interest in Hungarian and other Slavic music. In this piece of work, he intermingles elements of Hungarian folk melodies and rhythm among a liberation of dissonance, presenting a unique, modern twist for listeners to appreciate traditional sounds that have been preserved for generations.

A classic example of folk elements in Bartok's suite is the opening passage of the first movement, Allegretto. The listener hears a dance-like rhythm played by the left hand, and a clear, tonal melody in the right hand. However, there exists a gradual transition towards dissonance as the music changes in varying tempos.  Bartok allows the folk melody to playfully rise and fall as it becomes further blended with more remote overtones, thus demonstrating his expressionist flair to a traditional sound.

The fast, lightning passages of the third movement, Allegretto molto, is clear example of an expressionist element in this suite. The listener can barely make out a melody line that is hidden among a flurry of rapid, chordal progressions. There is a tease of a fleeting folk-like rhythm, but without a second, more-detailed consideration, the listener will simply miss the subdued folk elements present in the third movement.

Folk elements in Bartok's Suite, Opus 14 are more expressionistic and dissonant when compared to those that appear in his Improvisations, Opus 20. For example, in the fifth number of Improvisations, Opus 20, we continue to hear the prominence of the folk melody, and his stylistic additions to the original folk song continues to emphasize the listener's focus on the melody, providing a tonal, familiar experience to the listener. Bartok's use of folk elements in Suite, Opus 14 is more sporadic and unstructured; melodic lines are not easily discerned and are easily whisked away by more expressionist lines.
