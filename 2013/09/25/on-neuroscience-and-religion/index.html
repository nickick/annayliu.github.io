On Neuroscience and Religion

Anna Liu

CS & Bio Student at Duke

My freshman year of college was extremely hectic, especially when it came to defining my academic interests. Like all first-year undergraduates embracing the opportunities of a liberal arts education, I took courses ranging from Greek civilizations to multivariable calculus, from religion to neuroscience. I like to tackle academia with an interdisciplinary approach; considering a variety of perspectives can often lead to new insights on how a multitude of once disparate topics are interconnected.

Ever since Neuro 101, I became fascinated with the human brain. How does a series of complex biochemical processes enable the ethereal creation of the human mind? How does this organic machine enable us to feel, to think, and to love? How are we physiologically capable of such things as believing and having faith in a higher power?

Science and religion are often considered as antagonistic fields of study; proponents of religion argue that science destroys the wonder and marvel of religious experiences, and perhaps even common experiences once attributed to faith or a supernatural power.  Those in favor of science often claim that religion has failed to enlighten, if not misguided,  its believers. Very few stand in the middle, and assert that science and religion are intertwined paths, bringing us closer and closer to the ultimate truth with each new spiritual revelation and scientific discovery.

I wrote the following paper for an Academic Writing class during my freshman year at Duke University. Its title “Touched by God: A Neural Basis for the Pentecostal Spiritual Experience” reflects my attempt to provide a scientific explanation to a spiritual phenomena that led to the rise and proliferation of a modern Christian movement, Pentecostalism. I am not a religious person, but I hope to prove the following point through this piece of writing: understanding the science behind a spiritual phenomenon only makes believing – and faith – stronger.

This article was originally published in Deliberations: A Journal of First-Year Writing at Duke University.
