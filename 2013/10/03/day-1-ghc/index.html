Day 1 at the 2013 Grace Hopper Conference

Anna Liu

CS & Bio Student at Duke

This post was originally published on Duke at the 2013 Grace Hopper Conference found here.

It is so awesome to meet women in technology

This past summer, like a lot of undergraduates around the nation studying computer science, I did a software engineering internship in the Bay Area. Out of the many people that I had met in the technology industry, I met say, at most, five to six female engineers. Now, during my first day at Grace Hopper, my mind seems convinced that tech is all women.

This morning, I attended the keynote event featuring Sheryl Sandberg, current COO of Facebook, Maria Klawe, President of Harvey Mudd College, and Telle Whitney, President and CEO of the Anita Borg Institute. From their conversation, I grasped an understanding of the variety issues that women in technology encounter, whether she is a new college graduate looking to begin her career as a software engineer to an experienced professional balancing her career with motherhood. I find it unfortunate that the computing industry, initially pioneered by women, is so heavily male-dominated, but I heavily cherish the strong efforts that experienced female engineers and leaders in the tech industry are putting in to expose young women to the wonders of computer science. I definitely agree that women bring a fresh, if not more holistic, perspective of software development than their male counterparts, and having a strong support group will encourage women to be confident and assertive of their skills. As a side note, I will be adding Sheryl Sandberg's book, Lean In, onto my reading list.

In the afternoon, I also attended a panel regarding technology visionaries and learned about ways to assert your ideas in a large, established company. Though the panel was target towards women with ten plus years in industry, I still found the ideas discussed applicable to my current situation. To be a good technology visionary in a large company, one needs to keep up to date with new technology and to be able to understand the company vision and how one's idea can help the company increase its revenue.The panelists heavily emphasized the importance of communication: not only should a person be able to clearly articulate her idea, but she should also be able to implement that idea. I believe that what the speakers tried to illustrate during their panel can be summed up in the following: If a picture is worth a thousand words, then a prototype is worth millions.

The rest of the day was very hectic. At the career fair, I visited companies ranging from startups to established organizations to develop a better sense of their work culture and products. I connected with so many people as well, ranging from college seniors to experienced professionals in industry. I learned what it was like to work at fast-paced startups such as Stripe and Medium and the unique work cultures that define teams in well-known companies like Apple and Salesforce.com. I concluded the evening at the Amazon party with a smile on my face and an optimistic outlook on what is to come in the next two days.

If this was day one at the Grace Hopper Conference, I cannot wait to see what opportunities the next two days will unfold.
