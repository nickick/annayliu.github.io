Day 3 at the 2013 Grace Hopper Conference

My last day at Grace Hopper was focused on entrepreneurship. I attended several panels where women founders described the trials and tribulations they encountered while starting and growing their respective companies. They give aspiring entrepreneurs advice on how to think of creative ideas with business potential and how to look for resources and support during the fundraising and incubation processes.

I think these successful women entrepreneurs and venture capitalists gave a very realistic outlook on the journey a person decides to embark when she chooses to start her own company. The road is tough, filled with unexpected twists and turns. A founder must be sharp, open-minded, and flexible. She must be capable of adapting her ideas and business plans to customer and market needs. She must be able to put aside her ego and build a team consisting of those that are able to complement and extend her skills. She must not only be able to clearly articulate the problem that her company attempts to solve, but also expertly craft a product that addresses the problem experienced by potential customers. She must not waver in her vision and ideals during times of failure because the livelihood of those she leads in her company is directly dependent on her steadfast leadership.

The panelists heavily emphasized that the work life of an entrepreneur and founder does not begin at 9am and end at 5pm. During the early phases of building the company, her work life is her life. She must dedicate herself entirely to her cause, living and breathing her company's vision until she makes it into reality.

Final Thoughts

I would like to thank Duke's department of computer science for providing me with the opportunity to attend the Grace Hopper Conference. At this conference, I was able to surround myself and meet so many incredible women in computing, and hearing their success stories only furthered my confidence as a female software engineer. I was glad to have shared this experience with my fellow female CS majors at Duke, and I am grateful for the opportunities that have arisen for me at this conference.

Code like a girl.

Peace.
